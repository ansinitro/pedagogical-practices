Teacher's Master Guide

Definitions, Algorithms, and Answers for Discrete Math.

Quick Reference: Definitions

Term Definition Plain English
Divides (\( a \mid b \)) \( b = ak \) for some integer \( k \). "a fits perfectly into b".
Prime Integer \( p > 1 \) whose only divisors are 1 and \( p \). Can't be broken into smaller integer pieces.
GCD Largest integer \( d \) such that \( d \mid a \) and \( d \mid b \). The biggest building block shared by both numbers.
Congruence \( a \equiv b \pmod n \) if \( n \mid (a-b) \). "a and b land on the same spot on a clock of size n".

Section 1: The Division Algorithm

Goal: Given any integers \( a, b \), write \( a = bq + r \).

⚡ Algorithm Recipe
  1. Calculate \( q = \lfloor a/b \rfloor \). (Round down).
  2. Calculate \( r = a - bq \).
  3. Check: Is \( 0 \le r < |b| \)? If yes, done.

Student FAQ

Q: Why did we subtract 1 from the quotient for negative numbers?

A: Because we need a positive remainder.

Example: \( -7 \div 2 \). Calculator says -3.5.

  • If \( q=-3 \): \( 2(-3) = -6 \). Remainder to -7 is -1. (BAD)
  • If \( q=-4 \): \( 2(-4) = -8 \). Remainder to -7 is +1. (GOOD)

We always push "down" (left on number line) to ensure the gap is positive.

Section 2: GCD & Linear Combinations

Goal: Find \( \gcd(a,b) \) and solve \( ax + by = c \).

⚡ Algorithm Recipe: Extended Euclidean
  1. Forward Pass: Divide \( a \) by \( b \) repeatedly until remainder is 0.
    \( r_{last} \) is the GCD.
  2. Check: Does GCD divide \( c \)? If no, STOP (no solution).
  3. Backward Pass: Start with \( \text{GCD} = r_{n-2} - q \cdot r_{n-1} \).
    Subsitute remainders upwards until you have \( \text{GCD} = a(x) + b(y) \).
  4. Scale: Multiply \( x, y \) by \( c/\text{GCD} \) to get final solution.

Student FAQ

Q: Why shift the general solution (Task 17)?

A: To find the smallest positive solution. The math allows infinite solutions (\( x = x_0 + bt \)). We just pick the value of \( t \) that makes \( x \) small and positive because it's easier to read.

Section 3: Primes & Divisors (Task 1, 17)

Goal: Determine if \( n \) is prime and count its divisors \( d(n) \).

⚡ Algorithm Recipe: Primality Tests
  1. Sqrt Test: Check divisibility only up to \( \sqrt{n} \).
  2. Digit Sum: Sum digits. If sum is divisible by 3, \( n \) is divisible by 3.
  3. Fermat Factorization: If \( n = x^2 - y^2 \), then \( n = (x-y)(x+y) \). (Used for 9833).

Concept: Count of Divisors \( d(n) \)

  • \( d(n) = 2 \) means \( n \) has exactly 2 divisors (1 and itself). Definition of Prime.
  • \( d(n) = 3 \) means \( n \) has exactly 3 divisors. This only happens for squares of primes (\( p^2 \)).
    Ex: 4 (1,2,4), 9 (1,3,9), 25 (1,5,25).

Section 4: Modular Arithmetic

Key Theorems

  • Fermat's Little Theorem: \( a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p \) if \( p \) is prime.
    Use: Reducing huge exponents modulo a prime.

Q: How do I handle negative mods like \( -127 \pmod{85} \)?

A: Keep adding the modulus (85) until the number is positive.

\( -127 + 85 = -42 \)

\( -42 + 85 = 43 \)

So \( -127 \equiv 43 \pmod{85} \).

Section 5: Chinese Remainder Theorem

Goal: Solve for \( x \) giving specific remainders for different coprime moduli.

⚡ Algorithm Recipe: CRT

System: \( x \equiv a_1 \pmod{n_1}, \quad x \equiv a_2 \pmod{n_2} \)

  1. Calculate total modulus \( N = n_1 \cdot n_2 \).
  2. Find partial moduli \( N_1 = N/n_1, N_2 = N/n_2 \).
  3. Find inverses \( y_1 \) such that \( N_1 y_1 \equiv 1 \pmod{n_1} \).
  4. Final Formula: \( x = \sum a_i N_i y_i \pmod N \).
